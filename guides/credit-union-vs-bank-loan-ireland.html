Credit Union vs Bank Loan in Ireland: Which Is Cheaper?

Rates verified 25 June 2026 · Based on €10,000 over 5 years

For a €10,000 personal loan in Ireland, bank rates and credit union rates are closer than you might expect — but they're not equal, and the answer depends heavily on which credit union you can access. This guide uses real, current rate data from the Irish market to give you an honest comparison.

Why the credit union average doesn't tell the full story

The 10.42% ILCU average is a blunt instrument. It is derived from a survey of affiliated credit unions carried out in July/August 2025 — a snapshot of the national picture, not your credit union's current rate.

Individual credit union rates vary significantly by:

  • Which credit union you belong to. Some charge well below the national average; a small number charge close to the 12.68% APR legal maximum.
  • Loan amount. Many credit unions price by band. The same credit union may charge 8.84% on €10,000–€25,000 but a higher rate on smaller amounts.
  • Your savings record. Credit unions take your history of saving with them into account. A member with consistent savings over years will often be offered a better rate than a recent joiner.
  • Loan purpose. Some credit unions offer preferential rates on certain loan types (car loans, home improvement, etc.).

The practical implication: the average tells you roughly where the market sits, but you cannot use it to predict what your own credit union will offer you. You need to ask them directly.

When a credit union can beat a bank

At the best credit union rate we've confirmed — First Choice Credit Union at 8.84% APR for €10,000–€25,000 — a credit union is cheaper than AIB's standard 8.95% APR personal loan. The difference on €10,000 over 5 years is about €25 in total interest paid.

That's a real saving, but a modest one. And it only holds if your credit union's rate is below AIB's. The more compelling case for credit unions tends to be on smaller loans, where bank minimum amounts can leave a gap that credit unions fill at rates that are competitive for the amount.

Note: the three cheapest products in the table above — the SBCI green loans at 3.00%–4.23% — are bank products, not credit union products. They require an approved SEAI home energy grant and that 75% of the loan goes to qualifying energy upgrade works. If you qualify, no credit union can match them. The catch: credit union membership

You cannot walk into any credit union and take out a loan. Irish credit unions operate on a common bond — membership is tied to where you live, work, or in some cases worship or study. Each credit union defines its own bond, and you must be a member before you can borrow.

The practical steps:

  1. Find which credit unions you are eligible to join at ilcu.ie.
  2. Open a share (savings) account and make contributions — most credit unions require you to save with them for a period before you can borrow.
  3. Apply for a loan once you are an established member.

This means a credit union loan is not a quick option if you need to borrow immediately. Banks (and fintechs like Revolut) can approve and disburse funds within days without any prior relationship.

One potential advantage banks don't offer: dividends

Some credit unions pay an annual dividend to members on their savings balance. This is separate from the loan rate and is paid from the credit union's surplus after expenses. It is not guaranteed — the board votes on it each year and it can be zero — but where it is paid, it reduces the effective cost of membership and, indirectly, the net cost of borrowing.

Banks in Ireland do not pay dividends on savings accounts in any comparable way. This is a structural advantage of the credit union model, even if the amount is unpredictable.

Our verdict

For most standard personal loans, the best bank rates (PTSB at 7.20%, Bank of Ireland at 8.30%) are cheaper than the credit union national average (10.42%). But if you're a member of a well-priced credit union — or can join one — you may find a rate that beats some bank products, particularly if you have a strong savings history with them.

The right answer is: get a rate from your credit union and compare it against the banks before you commit. Use our full loan comparison table to see every lender's current rate, then ask your credit union what they would offer you personally. Don't decide based on the national average alone.
