How We Compare — Our Methodology

Last updated: [DATE] · Maintained by [EDITOR NAME], Editor

BorrowClever exists to answer one question honestly: for a given borrowing need, which Irish product actually costs you the least? This page explains exactly how we work that out — what we measure, where our numbers come from, how often we check them, and how we make sure our rankings aren't shaped by anything other than cost to you. We publish this in full because a comparison you can't audit isn't worth trusting.

Our one ranking principle: total cost of credit

We rank by the total cost of credit — the full amount the borrowing actually costs you over its life — not by the monthly repayment and not by the headline rate a lender leads with.

This matters because the two most common ways products are marketed are both misleading on cost. A lower monthly repayment usually just means a longer term and more interest paid overall. And a headline "rates from X%" figure is typically a best-case rate available only to some borrowers or only on certain loan sizes. We strip both away and compare the like-for-like total you'd repay.

How we compare personal loans

To compare loans fairly, every product has to be measured on the same loan. We use a standard benchmark of €10,000 repaid over 5 years, and we calculate each lender's total interest and total repayable on that exact loan using a consistent compound-interest calculation. Ranking is lowest total cost first.

Two points of discipline sit behind those numbers:

We use the rate that applies to the benchmark amount — not a lender's headline rate. Many Irish lenders price loans in bands by size, and the eye-catching rate they advertise often applies only to large loans (commonly €20,000 or €25,000 and above). A €10,000 borrower cannot get that rate. So for each lender we use the rate that genuinely applies to a €10,000 loan (the relevant smaller-loan band), even where it is higher than the figure the lender promotes. This is the single most common way loan comparisons mislead, and we treat it as a hard rule.

We flag conditions rather than hiding them. Where a rate is fixed or variable, where a "green" rate requires an SEAI grant or that the money go toward energy upgrades, or where a product is a credit-union loan with membership requirements, we say so on the row. A cheap rate with strings attached is not the same as a cheap rate, and we don't let the ranking pretend otherwise.

How we compare credit cards

Credit cards have no fixed term or balance, so total cost can't be expressed the same way as a loan. Instead we rank by representative APR, which is the standardised cost measure every Irish lender is required to quote. It assumes a €1,500 balance repaid over 12 months and includes the €30 annual Government Stamp Duty, so it captures interest and any annual card fee in one comparable figure.

Because the headline APR doesn't tell the whole story, we show two things alongside it:

Balance-transfer intro offers, in their own column. These are often the real reason people switch cards, yet they're frequently buried. We show each card's introductory offer explicitly — and we distinguish a genuine 0% offer from a reduced-rate one (for example a 2.9% or 3.83% intro rate), because they are not the same thing. We also note the duration, any transfer-window deadline, caps, and whether an offer is new-customer-only, so the headline "0%" is never read as more than it is.

Annual fees. Most Irish cards carry only the €30 stamp duty, but a few add an annual card fee that materially changes their true cost. We surface that rather than leaving it folded invisibly inside the APR.

Which products we include

Our tables are built for the general public, so we only list products that are broadly available to people who can apply for them — not deals restricted to a closed group such as students, or cards available only to members of one named organisation. Where a product carries a normal eligibility criterion that anyone can potentially meet, such as a minimum income, we don't exclude it, but we state that requirement clearly on the row so you know before you apply.

We aim to cover the full Irish market for each product type — the established banks, the fintechs and app-based providers, An Post Money, and credit unions — rather than a convenient subset. Where a well-known provider operates in Ireland but does not currently offer the product, we say so, so you aren't left wondering.

Where our data comes from

We take our figures directly from each lender's own published rates and from the Competition and Consumer Protection Commission (CCPC.ie), Ireland's official source for consumer financial information. We fetch lenders' rate pages directly rather than relying on third-party aggregators, because we have repeatedly found direct sources to be more current and more accurate. Every product records the source it was taken from and the date it was last checked.

How often we update

We review every rate on a fortnightly cycle, and each product carries a visible "last verified" date so you can see for yourself how current it is. We update sooner when a lender announces a change we become aware of between reviews. Rates and offers in the Irish market change without much notice — introductory offers expire, banks reprice, providers enter and leave — so we treat freshness as part of being accurate, not a nice-to-have.

If you ever find a figure that's out of date or wrong, please tell us at hello@borrowclever.ie — we'll check it against the lender and correct it, and corrections are made openly.

How money does — and doesn't — affect what you see

Our rankings are determined solely by cost to you. Commission, advertising, or any commercial relationship never changes the order of our tables, never promotes one product above a cheaper one, and never determines which products we include. The full detail of how we fund the site is set out on our How We Make Money page; the short version is that nothing on that page is allowed to override the principle on this one.
